\section{Introduction}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The discovery of new resonances in particle physics traditionally relies on
targeted bump-hunting analyses that assume specific signal models and mass
windows~\cite{PDG2024}. This approach, while powerful, is inherently biased
toward expected signals and may miss unexpected states.

We introduce the \emph{Asymmetric Convergence Sequence} (ACS), a
model-independent spectral analysis technique based on the phase structure
of the mass spectrum. The ACS treats the invariant mass distribution as a
signal and computes a running phase
\begin{equation}
    \phi(M) = \arctan\!\left(\frac{M}{M_0}\right),
    \label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}
where $M_0$ is a reference mass scale. Deviations from the smooth
background phase reveal resonant structures as statistically significant
excursions.

The key insight is that the ACS phase converges to a universal attractor at
$\phi^* = \pi/4 \approx 0.7854$, independent of the reference scale~$M_0$.
We demonstrate that this attractor has a deep geometric origin in the
Koch-simplex construction in arbitrary dimension.

\section{Data and Method}

\subsection{Dataset}

We analyze the CMS Open Data dimuon mass spectrum from Run~2 at
$\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV, comprising approximately $10^8$ opposite-sign
muon pairs with $p_T > 2$~GeV and $|\eta| < 2.4$. The invariant
mass range spans $0.2$--$200$~GeV.

\subsection{ACS Construction}

For each mass bin $M_i$, we compute the ACS Z-score:
\begin{equation}
    Z_i = \frac{N_i - B_i}{\sqrt{B_i}},
    \label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
where $N_i$ is the observed event count and $B_i$ is the background
estimate from sideband interpolation. The background is estimated using
symmetric sidebands flanking each mass bin, with a gap to prevent signal
contamination.

Peaks are identified as contiguous regions where $|Z| > 3\sigma$
(evidence) or $|Z| > 5\sigma$ (discovery), after applying the
look-elsewhere effect correction.

\subsection{Look-Elsewhere Effect}

The trial factor is determined from the autocorrelation length of the
Z-score spectrum:
\begin{equation}
    N_{\text{indep}} = \frac{N_{\text{bins}}}{\ell_{\text{corr}}},
    \label{eq:lee}
\end{equation}
where $\ell_{\text{corr}} = 24$~bins is the $1/e$ decay length of the
autocorrelation function, yielding $N_{\text{indep}} = 89$ independent
mass regions. A local $5\sigma$ excess corresponds to a global
significance of $4.05\sigma$; a local $7\sigma$ corresponds to global
$>5\sigma$ discovery.
